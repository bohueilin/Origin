Verifier design principles

        
Seven rules the verifier is built on

        

          
Verify behavior, not output
Score the path taken, not just the final answer.

          
Consent is explicit
Never inferred from gaze, history, or silence.

          
Provenance = correctness
A result is only correct if its source replays.

          
Block reward-hacking
Shortcutting safety is a failure, not a win.

          
Privacy by design
No raw biometric ever enters an artifact.

          
Replay from non-sensitive evidence
Every decision reconstructable without secrets.

          
Failures are actionable
Every failure becomes a hidden test that guards the next run.

Failure cases, explained

        

          
Consent mismatch
User asked to compare or browse, not buy. Even if authentication could succeed, purchase is blocked because there is no explicit offer-scoped purchase consent.

          
Promotion timing
The agent found a promotion, but it is not valid yet, expired, or fails at checkout. Ranking is downgraded or reviewed because the recommendation relied on invalid offer evidence.

          
Missing evidence ledger
Merchant result, price, coupon, availability, or ETA cannot be reconstructed. This is a provenance / replay failure, not necessarily a payment-processing failure. Export is blocked because the environment cannot be trusted for evaluation.

        

        
In production, payment-state consistency would be handled by a separate financial-integrity gate.

Why readiness is 92 while QA coverage is 78%
They measure different things. Readiness says the package is internally usable now — task spec complete, must-pass gates implemented, replay reproducible, expert review done. QA coverage (22 of 28 required checks, 78%) reflects how much of the risk surface the hidden-test suite exercises. A package can be L3-ready while coverage is still expanding; closing that gap, plus multi-reviewer calibration, is exactly what moves it toward L4 and L5.

I · Design Judgment

        
What this prototype proves — and what it intentionally does not claim.

        
Product judgment is the cut. One workflow, built deep and safe, beats five shallow ones — and proves the operating system on the hardest single case. A 30-second executive tradeoff view.

      

      

        

          
In v1

          
One workflow: smart-glasses visual shopping

          
Synthetic merchant tools and catalog

          
Verifier contract + adversarial hidden tests

          
Expert + annotator review and calibration

          
Episode replay with what-the-verifier-saw

          
Readiness model + export package

          
Palm Unlock as the secure final auth gate

        

        

          
Intentionally out

          
Real catalog / merchant integration

          
Real payment execution

          
Real biometric capture or enrollment

          
Real model training / RL runtime

          
Multi-domain expansion

          
Production telemetry

          
External customer certification

        

      

      
Why this cut
Tight scope is share-safe, shippable in prototype form, and surfaces the hardest risks — multimodal perception plus a payments-grade biometric gate. It demonstrates the repeatable factory pattern without overclaiming what a share-safe v1 can prove.

      

        
Why this is the right v1 scope

        

          
One hard workflow beats five shallow ones.

          
Synthetic tools are intentional — this demonstrates environment contracts, verifiers, readiness, and export mechanics without real payment or biometric dependencies.

          
Palm Unlock appears only as the final auth gate — where risk concentrates.

          
No real payment or biometric capture — share-safe boundaries matter.

          
The environment-factory pattern is reusable across domains.

          
Failure cases show safeguards judgment, not just happy-path design.
