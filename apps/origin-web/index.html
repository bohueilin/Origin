The problem

High-consequence agents get stuck before launch.

Agents are moving from chat into action — touching production systems, internal tools, infrastructure, code, PII, and money. But a reviewer can't approve what no one can bound or prove. So the launch date slips.

No boundary

No blast-radius bound

Nothing limits what the agent can reach or spend before it acts.

No owner

No approval owner

When a risky action needs a human, it's unclear who signs off — or whether they did.

No control

No side-effect path control

The agent can reach tools directly; there's no single checkpoint every action must pass.

No record

Scattered logs, no evidence

Prompts here, logs there, a screenshot in Slack — nothing packaged as review-ready evidence.

No proof

Can't prove what happened

After an incident, there's no tamper-evident record of what the agent did and why it was allowed.

Cost

The launch date slips

The workflow is ready; the approval isn't. The team ships spreadsheets and "trust us."

The Origin loop

Capability is not permission.

The agent proposes. A policy gate evaluates scope, budget, and approval rules. A single controlled proxy is the configured path to side effects. A human owns the risky calls. Every step lands in a tamper-evident trace — the proof the work was allowed, and what actually happened.

Where Origin starts

Built first for internal-ops and production-access agents.

Origin starts where the risk is concrete and the reviewer is one person: agents that touch production systems, internal tools, infrastructure, code, PII, or money-adjacent workflows. One wrong action has real blast radius — which is exactly where a gate, a proxy, and a tamper-evident record earn their keep. We widen from there through evidence, not press releases.

Why Origin

Spreadsheets, logs, and "trust us" don't get agents approved.

Identity and observability are adjacent jobs. Origin focuses on the approval path — enforcing what's allowed before side effects, then proving the decision afterward.

IdentityTells you who the agent is. Necessary — but it doesn't bound what the agent may do.
ObservabilityHelps engineers debug what happened. Built for debugging, not for approval.
OriginEnforces what's allowed before side effects — and produces a review-ready record of the decision, action, approver, and audit trail. Vendor-neutral across clouds and model providers.

Who's building it

Built by someone who has owned the launch gate.

Origin comes from ~20 years building the "prove it's safe before you ship" layer — payment-grade biometric authentication (designing the false-accept / false-reject operating points that let a face unlock a payment), frontier-safety launch gates and residual-risk ownership, and RL environments with verifier integrity. I've been the reviewer who couldn't approve a launch because there was no way to bound what a system could do or prove what it did. Origin is that missing layer, built for agents.

Origin starts with software agents. The longer arc is any high-consequence system where actions need policy, control, and proof — that's the vision, not today's product.
